Is “Swami” even a word? Richard sighed in frustration. Wordle must really be running out of decent five-letter candidates to have to resort to that one. No wonder he flamed out again; the New York Times editors were clearly reaching too deep into their Scrabble dictionary. Yesterday it was “Nymph” and the day before was some other Arabic-origin word he couldn’t remember that first broke his streak, adding up to three days of failure. Sad to see the Times losing the plot, but I guess all good things must come to an end. It just wasn’t fun anymore. Connections was really getting obscure, too. And it had been weeks since he had broken 40 seconds on the mini crossword.

Richard switched tabs to check the stock market in his Vanguard account, but it had logged him out. For some reason the password in his password manager wasn’t working either, and after three failed tries it told him he had to call customer service to get it reset. It was turning into that kind of morning. 

Actually, now that he looked, all his tabs were showing logged-out status. The laptop must have rebooted overnight and the cache wiped. What a hassle. His phone wasn’t unlocking with his face and he couldn’t remember the PIN, which was something long and complicated for some dumb reason. He probably wrote it down somewhere. Again, what a hassle. 

“Cythnia”, he called out to his wife. “Do you know where we wrote down the phone passwords?” 

She didn’t answer. He got up to look for her, but as he walked into the main hall, there were two men standing there in blue scrubs. “Who are you?” Richard asked with mounting irritation. 

“We’re from Veritas, delivering the service you requested” one of them said. 

“What? What service?  Cynthia\!”

Cynthia came down the stairs. “Richard, we talked about this. These gentlemen are from Veritas, and it’s time.”

“Time for what?”

“You’ve failed Wordle three days in a row, Connections twice and your crossword score has been consistently falling for nearly a year,” Cynthia said gently. “These biomarkers, along with others from your phone, the car and the smart speakers, confirm that you’ve got level 3 dementia. You are in decline, and it’s only going to get worse.” 

“We thought this day might come, so we prepared and you signed the form. Power of attorney has now transferred to me and we will start the euthanasia process you asked for.”

““Wait, you’re going to have me killed because I broke my Wordle streak?” Richard laughed. “You must be joking.”

“Richard, this breaks my heart,” she continued, now holding both his hands in hers. “But whether you remember it or not, this is what you wanted when you were of sound mind. Now, as painful as it is to realize, you’re not anymore. That’s why we set this up back then. You’re going to transition peacefully and without pain. We’ll remember you at your best.”

The two men moved forward to take his elbows and steer him to the door and the waiting ambulance. All he could think on the way out was that he was still wearing his slippers.
